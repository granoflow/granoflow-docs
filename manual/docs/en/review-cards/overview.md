You may already be used to thinking of "cards" as tools for memorizing words, concepts, or answers. That understanding isn't wrong, but it's not enough when it comes to GranoFlow.

A GranoFlow card is more like a note of experience you can carry with you: it comes from what you have done, and it should return to what you will do. It doesn't just remind you of "what the answer is," but more importantly reminds you of "how I should judge next time I encounter a similar situation."

For example, you've just finished a thesis proposal presentation and wrote during the review:

> What the professor really cared about was not how many materials I listed, but whether the research question could be clearly stated in one sentence.

If this sentence only stays in that day's review, it certainly has value, but you might not remember it later. Turning it into a card and linking it to the presentation task will allow it to reappear when you write abstracts, hold group meetings, or prepare for your defense in the future. Experience starts to transform from "something that happened that day" into "judgment you can use next time."

## Common Misunderstandings

The most common misunderstanding is: the more cards you have, the more solid your learning.

In reality, too many cards that are scattered and detached from tasks can turn you back into another collection box. You might flip through many cards every day, but rarely know where those contents should be used. Such cards are more like a pile of materials than experience.

GranoFlow deliberately places cards together with tasks, reviews, and projects to avoid this floating feeling. You can add a card from the task details, or see cards related to a task during your daily, weekly, or monthly review and then practice. The system cares not about how much content you bring in, but whether that content has a chance to return to real action.

## Core Concept: Cards Are the Smallest Reusable Unit of Experience

A review record can be long, containing that day's emotions, processes, background, and details. Cards are smaller—they only keep the bits worth extracting again later.

Those bits can be:

- A judgment principle: confirm what the other party is truly constrained by before communicating.
- A method reminder: when reading a paper, first find the problem, evidence, and conclusion; don't rush to copy sentences.
- A boundary of common mistakes: archiving is not deletion—it's just stepping back from active attention.
- An operational experience: when reviewing a project, first check whether the milestones still hold, then look at the number of tasks.

It's like compressing an experience into a bookmark that can be lit up again. The bookmark itself is not the book, but it can bring you back to an important place.

## A Real Task Example

Suppose you are a graduate student and today you completed the task "Organize the interview guide." During the review, you realized that you wrote many questions initially, but the truly useful ones were those that could elicit specific experiences from the interviewees.

You can turn this experience into a card:

- Title: Interview questions should elicit specific experiences
- Front: When designing interview questions, how do you avoid getting only abstract evaluations?
- Back: Change "How do you see it?" to "Last time this happened, what did you do, what constraints were there, and what changed afterward?"

This card is not for you to memorize a nice phrase. Its value lies in the fact that next time you create tasks like "Prepare for user interview," "Write research questions," or "Review interview results," you can associate this card with them and practice again through the task or review context.

## Reusable Judgment Principles

To decide whether a piece of content is suitable for a card, ask three questions:

1. Will this experience be encountered again in the future?
2. Can it help me make better judgments in my next task?
3. Can it be compressed into a clear question and a usable answer?

If the answers are all "yes," it's worth entering as a card. Conversely, if it's just an emotional outburst of the day, temporary facts, or trivial records that won't be used again, leave it in the review.

That's also why GranoFlow does not encourage you to import a large number of cards in bulk at the beginning. Cards are best grown slowly from real tasks. Fewer, but each knows where it came from and where it needs to return.

## Where to Enter the Card System

Cards usually have several entry points:

1. In the task details of a project, click "Add card" in the "Task Cards" area.
2. On the "Linked Cards" page, search for and link existing cards, or choose "AI Add Card" or "Add Card."
3. In the "Card Learning" area of the progress page, access card statistics, practice, or management.
4. In daily, weekly, or monthly reviews, enter contextual practice from the related task cards.

These entry points may seem scattered, but they all serve the same purpose: keeping cards in the context of tasks and reviews instead of turning into another isolated repository.

## Boundaries: Cards Are Not Complete Records

Cards do not replace tasks, reviews, or project documentation.

Tasks specify what you need to do; reviews record how you see things after they happen; projects carry long-term goals. Cards only take the part that is most suitable for repeated use later. The clearer they are, the easier they will be remembered in the future. The more they try to cover everything, the more they become long texts no one wants to review.

A simple self-check is: if next time you see this card you can immediately know which type of task it applies to, it's a good card. If you need to re-read a large chunk of background to understand what it says, it might be better to sort it out in the review or project documentation rather than rushing to make a card.
