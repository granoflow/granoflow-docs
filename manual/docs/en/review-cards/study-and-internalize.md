Card practice can easily feel tense. You see a question, flip to the answer, then tap "Forgot" or "Easy" — as if the system is grading you.

In GranoFlow, it's better to think of it as a very short reminder: Do I still remember this experience? Do I know where it applies? When I encounter a similar task next time, can I make a quicker judgment?

Practice is not about proving you have a good memory; it's about giving experience a chance to move from the card back into action.

## Misconception: Practicing easily means you've learned it

If you can answer a card today, it only means it is easy to retrieve today. It doesn't necessarily mean you can use it in a real task.

For example, you might be able to recite:

> Interview questions should elicit concrete experiences.

That's good, but not enough. What's truly useful is when you next write an interview guide and naturally change "What do you think?" to "The last time it happened, what did you do?" That's when a card moves from memory to use.

GranoFlow uses "Mastered" and "Internalized" to distinguish these two levels. Mastered means it's stable in review; Internalized means it has been brought back to tasks in multiple different projects and is now participating in real action as experience.

## Core Concepts: Active Review vs. Contextual Review

"Card Learning" on the Progress page is active review. It uses today's due-for-practice and review schedule to remind you which active cards should be practiced.

Card practice within tasks, daily reviews, weekly reviews, and monthly reviews is contextual review. It doesn't just ask "Is it due today?" but rather "What relevant experiences in this task or review are worth revisiting?"

Both types of practice matter:

- Active review helps maintain memory, preventing experience from sinking completely.
- Contextual review helps you place experience back into a scenario, seeing whether it can guide the current task.

If active review is like regularly flipping through bookmarks, contextual review is like discovering that an old bookmark is just right for the new chapter you're writing.

## A Real Task Example

You have a card:

- **Front**: When designing interview questions, how do you avoid getting only abstract evaluations?
- **Back**: Ask the person to describe a recent real experience, including the constraints at the time, the actions taken, and the changes that followed.

The first time you practice, you might tap "Hard". After a few more practices, you can answer it stably, and it moves to Mastered.

But the real change comes later. In three different projects — "Thesis Interview", "Product User Research", and "Team Retrospective Interview" — you link this card to tasks and use it when preparing questions. At that point, the system will classify it as Internalized: it is not just remembered, but has been used across different projects.

Internalized is not an AI automatic evaluation, nor does it mean that card never needs review again. It's just a very useful indicator: this experience has left the card deck and returned to your actions.

## Four Learning States

Card learning states are divided into four categories:

- **Unlearned**: No review record yet, or no learning state.
- **Learning**: Has been reviewed but not yet reached Mastered.
- **Mastered**: Stable in review but does not yet meet the Internalized conditions.
- **Internalized**: Mastered, and the same card has been linked to tasks in 3 different projects.

Multiple tasks within the same project count as only one project. This limitation is important because internalization is about cross-context transfer, not repeated occurrence within the same project.

## How to Rate During Practice

The practice page first shows the question. After you tap to reveal the answer, you give feedback using four levels:

- **Forgot**: Basically can't recall.
- **Hard**: Some impression but not stable, or needed to see the answer to connect.
- **Good**: Could answer, understood basically clearly.
- **Easy**: Very natural, almost no effort.

These four levels are used by the system to schedule future reviews. Don't choose a higher level just to make the statistics look good, and don't blame yourself if you're having a bad day. The more accurate your feedback, the more helpful the subsequent reminders.

If today's due-for-practice is 0, but there are still candidate cards for practice, the Progress page may show "Today's card practice is complete" and "Practice another set". This is for when you still have energy to continue with a small group; it doesn't mean you must finish extra today.

## Viewing Notes

Some cards belong to a more complete note. The practice page provides a note entry point; opening it lets you view in a side panel:

- Note title and content
- Corresponding translation
- Source
- Linked projects and linked tasks
- All cards under the same note

Linked projects are shown with three dots indicating coverage of 0, 1, 2, 3 or more projects; tasks without a project are placed under "Uncategorized Projects". When you tap a linked task, GranoFlow will first close the note panel, then open the task details.

The purpose of this panel is not for you to read long text during practice, but to help you retrieve context when needed: where this card came from, which tasks it is related to, and which other cards exist under the same note.

## Archiving and Trash

Archiving is suitable for cards you no longer want to enter active review, but still want to keep for preservation.

Archived cards do not appear in the active review card count, today's due-for-practice, or active review queue on the Progress page; however, they still retain content, task associations, and review context. You may still see them in related tasks or reviews, and you can unarchive them from the archived view if needed.

Moving to trash is different. It means the card is temporarily deleted: it no longer appears in normal card details, learning queues, or associated card areas. As long as the trash is not emptied, you can restore it; after permanent deletion or emptying the trash, you cannot rely on the trash to recover it.

By default, internalized cards are protected in the card management list. When you swipe to archive or move to trash, the system will remind you that this card has been used in multiple projects. This reminder is not to stop you from organizing, but to prevent you from accidentally deleting experience that has proven useful.

## Entering Practice from the Progress Page

The "Card Learning" area on the Progress page shows the active review card count and today's due-for-practice number. Tapping the total card count lets you enter card statistics, where you can view learning states, the load for the next 7 days, and recent practice activity.

Card statistics is the main entry point to the card series page. From there you can enter card practice, or open card management. Card practice and card management open as sub-pages and return to the card statistics page.

## A Quick Check

After practicing a card, you can quickly ask yourself:

- Do I only remember the answer, or do I know what type of task it applies to?
- Should it continue active review?
- Is it already outdated and should be archived or moved to trash?
- Is there a real task recently that I can link it to?

These questions don't need to be written down every time. They just help you pull practice back from "tapping buttons" to "what to do next".
