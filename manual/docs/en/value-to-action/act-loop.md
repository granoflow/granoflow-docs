This chapter turns ACT and The Happiness Trap into a GranoFlow practice: domains, values, projects, milestones, tasks, and review. Use it to turn long-term goals into one task you can start today, without making task management another source of anxiety.

One important idea behind GranoFlow is ACT: Acceptance and Commitment Therapy.

In simple terms, ACT does not ask you to eliminate anxiety, procrastination, pain, or confusion before you start living. It asks a different question: while those states are still present, can you still notice what matters to you and take the next action?

Many people first meet ACT through Russ Harris's *The Happiness Trap*. The book explains ACT in everyday language: you do not need to remove every painful feeling, anxious thought, procrastination pattern, or uncertainty before you can begin living toward what matters.

ACT is commonly discussed within the broader family of modern cognitive behavioral therapies (CBT), with attention to acceptance, mindfulness, values, and committed action. Its practical focus is not to erase every uncomfortable feeling. It is to build psychological flexibility: notice what is happening now, notice what you value, and take an action that moves you closer to that value.

GranoFlow is not a therapy tool and does not replace books, doctors, therapists, or professional help. It only borrows the part of ACT and *The Happiness Trap* that fits everyday life: accepting reality, clarifying values, committing to action, and leaving a trail through tasks, projects, and review.

## Acceptance: Start by Acknowledging the Current State

Many people assume they can only act after their state improves.

Wait until I am not anxious, then work.  
Wait until I stop procrastinating, then study.  
Wait until I understand everything, then plan.  
Wait until life is stable, then start changing.

Real life usually does not work that way.

You may feel confused and still have things to do. You may doubt yourself and still want to move a goal forward. You may not feel fully ready, but today still requires a beginning.

In GranoFlow, the first step is not to make yourself perfect. It is to write the thing down.

Writing something down does not mean you have understood it.  
Writing something down only means you acknowledge that it is taking up your attention.

You can put a task into the inbox, or write today's state in Review. It does not need to be explained, categorized, or optimized yet. Recording it moves the mess from your head into a place where you can work through it slowly.

Acceptance means: I acknowledge that this is where I am, and I start from here.

## Values: Understand What Matters to You

Tasks answer, "What do I need to do?"

Values answer, "What kind of person do I want to become?"

The same English study plan can come from an exam, a job, or the wish to understand the world more freely.  
The same workout can be about appearance, health, or having more strength for the long run.  
The same project can be about income, craft, or proving to yourself that you can finish something over time.

Domains and values in GranoFlow are not there to decorate the page.

They help you see whether the places where you repeatedly spend time are actually closer to the directions you care about.

You do not need perfect life values on day one. Start with a few simple questions:

- What kind of person do I want to be in work or learning?
- How do I want to treat important relationships?
- How do I want to care for my physical and mental health?
- What work, expression, or creation do I want to leave behind?

The answers can be ordinary.

- I want to be reliable.
- I want to keep moving even when things are difficult.
- I want to create something, not only consume life.
- I want to care for my body instead of always overusing it.

Useful values are often not polished slogans. They are directions that can guide repeated choices.

## Commitment: Turn Direction into the Next Action

Writing values is not enough.

If "I want to be reliable" stays only as a sentence, it will not change your life by itself. It needs to become projects, milestones, and tasks.

For example, you value being reliable.

That can become a project:

> Finish the current product version

The project can have milestones:

> Finish core features  
> Finish testing  
> Prepare release materials

Those milestones can become tasks:

> Check the sign-in flow  
> Fix image upload issues  
> Update manual copy  
> Submit review notes

Now the value is no longer just an abstract wish. It has entered a structure you can move forward each day.

Committed action does not mean, "I can never pause again."

It means: even when my state is not perfect, I am willing to take one concrete action toward a direction I care about.

When you do not know what to do, look at the task.  
When tasks feel too scattered, look at the milestone.  
When a project loses meaning, look at the value.  
When the value becomes blurry, return to Review and reorganize it.

## Review: Let Experience Become Accumulation

Completing a task is not the end.

If you only keep writing tasks, doing tasks, and deleting tasks, life can turn into a list that gets consumed and disappears.

Review helps experience leave a trace.

At the end of the day, you can ask yourself:

- What did I actually do today?
- What moved me closer to a direction I care about?
- What only drained me?
- What should the next step be?

Review does not need to be long or polished. It is not an essay, a work report, or self-criticism.

It is a short reset: reconnect what happened today with the direction you care about over time.

Sometimes Review tells you that a project is worth continuing.  
Sometimes it tells you that a goal no longer matters.  
Sometimes it only helps you admit that today was hard, and you still did one thing.

All of that counts.

## Pausing Is Not Failure

GranoFlow does not make streaks the core.

Life gets interrupted.

You may get sick, change jobs, feel low, deal with something urgent, or simply lose momentum. A pause does not mean you failed. It means life changed.

What matters is not "I never stopped." What matters is "I can come back after stopping."

When you come back, you do not need to compensate for the past or blame yourself. You only need to look again:

- Which domain still matters now?
- Which project is still worth continuing?
- What is the nearest milestone?
- What is the smallest task I can move forward today?

If the old plan no longer fits, adjust it, archive it, or let it go.

Letting go of a project does not cancel the effort you already put in. If you can see what you learned, it can still become part of your experience.

## Complete One ACT Loop with GranoFlow

You can think of one complete use of GranoFlow as a simple loop:

1. **Accept reality**: write down the mess, task, or state in your head.
2. **Notice values**: decide which domain, value, or long-term direction it relates to.
3. **Commit to action**: turn the direction into projects, milestones, and tasks.
4. **Move forward**: complete one clear next step today.
5. **Review and settle**: record what happened and what the next step is.
6. **Return after pausing**: do not reset or shame yourself; start again from the current state.

You do not need to complete this whole loop every day.

Sometimes you only write one thing down.  
Sometimes you only complete one task.  
Sometimes you only review.

Those are all valid ways to use the system.

GranoFlow is not trying to turn you into someone who is always efficient. It helps you keep moving closer to what matters in real life.

## Further Reading

If you want to learn more about ACT, you can read Russ Harris's *The Happiness Trap*. You can also read the Wikipedia pages for [The Happiness Trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Happiness_Trap) and [Acceptance and commitment therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceptance_and_commitment_therapy), or the introduction to ACT from the Association for Contextual Behavioral Science.

GranoFlow is not a summary of *The Happiness Trap*, a substitute for the book, or a therapy product. It only turns part of the idea that fits daily life into a structure for notes, projects, tasks, and review, so "what matters to me" can become one step you can take today.
