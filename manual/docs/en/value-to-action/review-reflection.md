Many people record tasks, complete them, and check them off. But if things end there, a day can easily reduce to two feelings:

- Didn't do enough
- Still have a lot left undone

That's why many task tools make you busier and busier, and more likely to feel like you're always falling behind.

The "review" in GranoFlow isn't meant to put your day on trial again.
It exists to help you gradually turn what happened from "completion records" into "experiences you can take with you."

Writing tasks is to lighten the mental load.
Completing tasks is to move things forward in reality.
Reviewing is about turning experiences into judgment, so you slowly learn:

- What works for you
- What only looks busy
- Which actions actually get closer to what you value
- How to see the next step more clearly

If you're familiar with ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) or *The Happiness Trap*, you can think of a review as a gentle "committed action check":

> Over this period, am I still moving toward what I truly value?

For more background, read [ACT and The Happiness Trap](/manual/en/value-to-action/act-loop/).

## A Review Is Not Self-Criticism

A review is not about criticizing yourself.

It's not asking:

> Why wasn't I hardworking enough today?
> Why did I procrastinate again?
> Why didn't I finish everything I planned?

These questions easily turn a review into pressure, and make you instinctively avoid it.

Better questions to ask are:

> What really happened today?
> What did I accomplish?
> Which actions are close to the direction I value?
> What needs adjustment?
> What's the next step?

The purpose of a review is not to judge yourself, but to see reality clearly.
Once you see reality, you know which things are worth continuing, which need to change, and which can be let go.

## Completing a Task Is Just the First Step

After a task is completed, it becomes a record.
But a record isn't yet an experience.

For example, you completed:

> Write the "Tasks and Inbox" chapter

If you just check it off, it's just a completed task.

If you write in your review:

> Finished writing the tasks chapter today. The structure is clear, but the "review" chapter needs a clearer distinction between "settling experiences" and "self-criticism." Will continue working on the review system tomorrow.

Then it becomes an experience.

Completing a task tells you: what I did.
A review tells you: what that means.

That's the real value of a review:
It lets you not just "spend" a day, but "keep" a day.

## A Few Questions Each Day Are Enough

A daily review doesn't need to be long.
Often, what makes a review sustainable is that it's short, honest, and without performance.

At the end of each day, you can just answer a few questions:

- What did I accomplish today?
- Which thing was closest to the direction I value?
- What consumed me but didn't really move anything forward?
- What's worth remembering?
- What's the next step?

You don't need to produce "profound insights" every day.
Sometimes a simple note is enough:

> Today was an average day, but I still completed one key task. I'll continue with the rest tomorrow.

That's also a valid review.

The point of a review is never eloquence, but honesty.

## Don't Just Look at Task Count

Completing many tasks doesn't necessarily mean the day was valuable.

Sometimes you finish ten small tasks, but they're all chores.
Sometimes you finish only one thing, but it truly advanced a project.

So when reviewing, don't just look at the count.
More importantly, look at:

- Which tasks advanced a project?
- Which tasks moved a milestone?
- Which actions aligned with your values?
- Which things just made you look busy?

GranoFlow's review is not to prove you were productive.
It's to help you see: whether your time actually flowed toward what matters.

## Keep the Experience and Review It Later

Some review content is useful beyond the day itself.

For example:

> Before communicating with a client, first write down the constraints they really care about.

Such experiences will be used again and again. You can organize them into knowledge cards and keep them associated with relevant tasks. Later, when you see these cards in task details, daily reviews, weekly reviews, or monthly reviews, you can enter card review and bring past experiences back into your next action.

This brings cards from "isolated memorization" back into real scenarios. In terms of learning levels, simply swiping cards leans toward recall. When you place cards alongside the tasks you're working on, you move faster toward understanding and application: you don't just remember the answer, you also judge where it applies, and you use it in the next action.

If you want to systematically understand how to create, associate, practice, archive, migrate, and import cards, continue reading [Cards: Bringing Experience Back into Action](/manual/en/review-cards/). This chapter only keeps the relationship between review and cards: review helps you discover experience, cards help you bring reusable experience back into your next task.

## Review Projects, Not Just the Day

In addition to daily reviews, also review projects regularly.

Because people easily fall into a state:

> Busy every day, but the project hasn't really progressed.

Project reviews can help you avoid this.
You can ask:

- Is this project still important?
- Is the current milestone clear?
- Which tasks really advanced it?
- Which tasks were just detours?
- Does this project still align with my values?
- Should the next phase be continued, adjusted, archived, or abandoned?

If a project hasn't progressed for a long time, it's not necessarily because you're lazy.
It might be too large, too vague, or no longer important.

What to do then is not to blame yourself, but to reorganize.

## After a Break, Resume with a Review

Life gets interrupted.

You might not open GranoFlow for a few days.
Or a project might be paused for weeks.
Or your earlier tasks may have expired.

Don't try to catch up.
Don't attempt to fill in every missed day. That only adds pressure and makes it harder to return.

A better approach is to do a brief review:

> What happened during this period?
> What things are no longer important?
> Which projects are still worth continuing?
> What's the smallest step I can restart with today?

A break is not failure.
What really matters is: after coming back, you can still see your direction and make the next move.

Here, the review is not about "handing in overdue homework,"
but gently helping you regain a sense of action.

## A Review Also Helps You Let Go

A review isn't just about continuing.
It also helps you let go.

Some tasks no longer need to be done.
Some projects are no longer worth pursuing.
Some goals were just ideas from an earlier stage.
Some plans sound right but no longer fit who you are now.

Without a review, they keep occupying your attention.

You can write in your review:

> This project no longer fits my current direction, so I'll archive it. The earlier attempt confirmed that I now need to focus on another project.

This is not failure.
It's turning past effort into judgment.

Often, what truly relieves you is not "finally finishing everything,"
but "finally seeing clearly that some things can stop."

## How Review Helps You Calibrate Your Values

Values aren't set and forgotten.
They need to be verified in review.

You can check your recent actions against your values to see if they truly draw closer.

For example, you wrote:

> I want to be a reliable person who consistently delivers.

In your review, ask:

> Have I actually been delivering recently?
> Did I spend a lot of time on preparation, hesitation, and repeated revisions?
> How can I get closer to "reliable delivery" next?

Another example, you wrote:

> I want to take care of my body long-term, not keep overdrawing it.

In your review, ask:

> Have I given my body room to recover recently?
> Do I only pay attention to my body when something goes wrong?
> What small adjustment can I make tomorrow?

Values provide direction; review checks whether that direction is still happening in reality.
So review is not a side activity – it's the loop that closes "value → action."

## Let AI Organize, but Don't Let It Judge for You

You can export or copy review content to an external AI (Artificial Intelligence) and let it help organize what you did this week, which projects advanced, and which tasks repeatedly got delayed.

AI is good at organizing, summarizing, and reminding; the final judgment should still be yours. Because only you know: which things really matter, and which things only look reasonable.

## A Simple Review Template

If you don't know where to start, you can use this template directly:

```text
What did I accomplish today?

Which thing was closest to the direction I value?

What difficulties or deviations did I have today?

What did I learn?

What's the next step?
```
